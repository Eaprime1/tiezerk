# Spacetime Sea — Navigation

*Practical protocols for traversing the temporal medium.*

---

## Before You Enter

1. **Assess the tide** — observe the current direction at the shore before entering; entering against the tide is possible but costly
2. **Identify your target depth** — surface navigation (present-proximate) vs. deep navigation (historical strata) require different preparation
3. **Note current luminescent markers** — significant nearby events emit light; orient yourself relative to these before descent

---

## Surface Navigation (Present-Proximate)

The surface of the Spacetime Sea is the most accessible layer. Navigation here is relatively stable, though temporal currents can accelerate unexpectedly.

- Move with currents where possible; fighting currents exhausts navigators quickly
- Use luminescent event-markers for orientation; they are the clearest fixed reference points at the surface
- Avoid regions of visible surface turbulence — these indicate dense event-mass below, causing spatial curvature that can disorient

---

## Depth Navigation (Historical Strata)

Diving into the historical strata requires preparation and caution.

- **Slow the descent** — rapid descent bypasses navigable strata and risks surfacing in compressed or inverted time zones
- **Track luminescence** — event-lights dim with depth; carry a resonance anchor to maintain orientation
- **Watch for inversion zones** — strata near high-mass historical events fold; what appears to be deeper may be shallower in temporal terms
- **Time dilation awareness** — time moves slower at depth; subjective time in the deep is not equivalent to surface time; plan accordingly

---

## Ascent Protocol

- Ascend gradually; rapid ascent from depth causes temporal disorientation (surfacing in the wrong present-moment)
- Return by tracking the resonance of your entry point; do not navigate by landmarks alone at depth
- If disoriented, pause — let the Sea's current carry you toward the surface; fighting disorientation increases it

---

## Hazard Regions

| Hazard | Description | Advisory |
|---|---|---|
| **Closed strata** | Regions sealed by pre-cycle forces; entry is blocked | Do not attempt entry; observe boundary only |
| **Inversion folds** | Strata folded by event-mass; past and future are locally reversed | Navigate with extreme caution; avoid if possible |
| **Temporal eddies** | Circular currents trapping navigators in repeating temporal loops | Exit perpendicular to the eddy's rotation axis |
| **Void-tube intersections** | Points where Void Marrow tubes cross the Sea | Exercise caution; exit destination is uncertain |

---

## Orientation Tools

- **Resonance anchor** — a personal frequency marker that stays at the surface while you dive; provides a return vector
- **Luminescence map** — a chart of known event-lights at various strata; updated as new significant events occur
- **Tidal calendar** — records the Sea's tidal pattern over a standard cosmic cycle; useful for predicting current direction

## Doldrums and Aldus

A doldrum is not merely stillness.

Stillness can be sacred when it remains alive. A vessel may hold position to heal, listen, grieve, or wait for the tide.

A doldrum becomes Aldus when:

1. rest no longer answers motion;
2. comfort becomes refusal;
3. pressure no longer transforms;
4. grief stops circulating;
5. the vessel mistakes entombment for peace.

In Altum, ascent and descent are not always vertical.

The strongest ascent does not abandon the deep. It carries the deep forward.
